## What is NQ?

NQ is the E-mini Nasdaq-100 futures contract, traded on the CME. One Mini NQ contract controls a 20× multiplier on the Nasdaq-100 index. There's also MNQ (Micro NQ), 1/10 the size, which is what most retail traders start with because it lets you take real positions with a few thousand dollars of margin instead of $20K+.

NQ is one of the most liquid index futures in the world. Tight spreads, 23-hour-a-day trading, and enough daily volatility (typically 100-300 index points) to work setups that don't require hours of screen time.

## What are "signals"?

A trading signal is a specific instruction to enter or exit a position. A good signal has:

1. A precise **entry** price (not "buy the dip" — an actual number)
2. A precise **stop loss** (the price that says "I was wrong, get out")
3. A precise **target** (the price that says "take the win")
4. A clear **time window** (most intraday signals expire in hours, not days)

A useful signal is one you could hand to an algorithm or to a trader who's never heard of you, and they'd execute the exact same trade you would.

## Systematic vs discretionary signals

Most retail "signal" services are discretionary — a human trader posts their gut calls in a Discord, and subscribers copy them. The problem is that discretionary signals are inconsistent: the trader has good days and bad days, sticks to rules sometimes and improvises other times, and there's no way to validate the approach against history.

A **systematic** signal is generated by a fixed set of rules applied to market data. Same rules every day, same entry logic, same exit logic. The advantage: you can backtest the rules against 10+ years of historical data and see exactly how the strategy would have performed. You know the win rate, the max drawdown, the profit factor, and the worst losing streak in advance.

## The step-by-step for trading NQ signals

Here's the actual workflow, assuming you subscribe to a signal service (ours or any other):

### Step 1 — Confirm your broker supports NQ or MNQ

Most major US brokers do: Interactive Brokers, Tradovate, TradeStation, NinjaTrader, TD Ameritrade, Charles Schwab. Check that your account is enabled for futures trading (usually requires a separate application) and funded to at least $5,000 if you're trading Micro, or $25,000+ for Mini.

### Step 2 — Decide your position size BEFORE you need it

This is the step most retail traders skip. Size too big and one bad streak wipes you out; size too small and you leave money on the table. A sensible starting rule: risk no more than 1% of your account on any single trade. If you have $10,000 and the signal's stop is $500 away from entry, that's a 1 Micro contract position (1 MNQ point ≈ $2, so 500 points ≈ $1,000 risk — that's already over your limit, so either wait for a tighter setup or downsize).

Most systematic signal services will give you a position-sizing calculator. Use it.

### Step 3 — Receive the signal

How the signal arrives matters. Dashboard + email is the minimum; Discord-only is a problem because Discord goes down, notifications get buried, and you can't verify timestamps. At STS Futures the signal fires simultaneously to the web dashboard and via email, and each signal is cryptographically timestamped so there's no ambiguity about when it was published.

A real-time signal should include:
- **Symbol:** NQ or MNQ
- **Direction:** Long or Short
- **Entry price** (exact number)
- **Stop loss** (exact number)
- **Target** (exact number, or a rule-based exit like "close at 15:55")
- **Quantity guidance** (what the signal was tested at; you adjust for your account size)

### Step 4 — Execute in your broker

This is the boring part. Open your trading platform, enter a bracket order (buy limit at entry + stop-loss + take-profit), and let it work. A typical intraday signal lives for 5 minutes to a few hours. If the entry doesn't fill within the signal's time window, the trade is void — don't chase.

### Step 5 — Review the outcome

After the trade closes (either the stop or target hits, or the time-based exit triggers), you record the outcome. Did it hit the target? Did it stop out? How far in the red did it go before coming back to target? Over weeks and months, you build a personal log that should closely match the service's published performance. If your actual performance diverges significantly from the backtest, that's a sign you're not executing consistently.

## The rules I wish someone had told me early

After trading NQ systematically for over a decade, these are the rules that separate profitable subscribers from the ones who blow up.

1. **Don't skip trades that look ugly.** The worst thing you can do with a systematic signal is cherry-pick the "obvious" setups and skip the uncertain ones. The signal's backtested edge comes from taking *all* the setups, winners and losers alike.
2. **Don't double-size winners.** "This one looks like a good setup, I'll trade two contracts instead of one" is how you blow up. Size is decided once based on account size, not on signal strength.
3. **Set a personal max drawdown.** If your account is down 15% from peak, step down to half-size until you recover. If you're down 25%, stop trading until you've investigated why the strategy drifted from backtest.
4. **Execute the exit rules exactly.** "The target is $22,400 but NQ is screaming higher, I'll let it run" — don't. The exit rules are part of the edge. Bending them breaks the statistical properties of the strategy.
5. **Don't add discretionary filters.** "I only take signals when VIX is below 20" sounds smart, but if the backtest didn't test that filter, you're blind to how it changes the distribution of returns. Changes to a strategy require a fresh backtest, not on-the-fly adjustments.
6. **Budget for drawdowns.** The 51% max drawdown in the 15-year record didn't happen in 2011 — it was in a later year after the account had already compounded significantly. Periodic peak-to-trough drawdowns of 20-30% are normal and should not trigger a strategy change.
