### Key Risks

#### 1. Leverage Risk

Futures trading involves leverage, which means you can control a large position with a relatively small amount of capital. While leverage can amplify profits, it can also amplify losses. A small adverse price movement can result in substantial losses or even the complete loss of your initial investment.

#### 2. Market Risk

Futures prices can be highly volatile and subject to rapid fluctuations due to:

- Economic data releases
- Geopolitical events
- Market sentiment changes
- Supply and demand imbalances
- Central bank policy decisions

#### 3. Liquidity Risk

While major futures contracts are generally liquid, liquidity can dry up during market stress, making it difficult to execute trades at favorable prices or exit positions.

#### 4. Operational Risk

- System failures or connectivity issues may prevent timely trade execution
- Broker platform outages could result in inability to manage positions
- Incorrect order entry or execution could result in unintended trades

#### 5. Counterparty Risk

Your trades depend on the solvency and operational integrity of your broker and clearing house. While regulated brokers are required to maintain capital reserves, there is always some counterparty risk.

#### 6. Model Risk

Our trading strategies are based on historical backtested data and algorithmic models. These models may not perform as expected in live trading due to:

- Market regime changes
- Structural market changes
- Overfitting to historical data
- Unexpected correlations between assets

#### 7. Technology Risk

Automated trading systems depend on:

- Reliable internet connectivity
- Broker API availability
- Server uptime and performance
- Correct algorithm implementation

Any of these could fail, resulting in missed trades or unintended executions.

#### 8. Regulatory Risk

Futures markets are subject to regulatory changes that could affect:

- Position limits
- Trading hours
- Margin requirements
- Contract specifications

### Specific Strategy Considerations

Our strategies are designed for intraday and short-term trading of E-mini futures contracts. Key considerations:

- **Slippage**: Actual execution prices may differ from backtested prices
- **Commissions**: Broker commissions and fees reduce net profitability
- **Gaps**: Price gaps between market close and open can result in unexpected losses
- **Correlation Changes**: Historical correlations may not persist
- **Market Conditions**: Strategies may perform differently in trending vs. range-bound markets

### Risk Management Recommendations

1. **Start Small**: Begin with paper trading or minimal position sizes
2. **Use Stop Losses**: Always use appropriate stop-loss orders to limit downside risk
3. **Position Sizing**: Never risk more than 1-2% of your account on a single trade
4. **Diversification**: Don't concentrate all capital in a single strategy
5. **Monitor Actively**: Regularly review your positions and account performance
6. **Understand Leverage**: Only use leverage you fully understand and can afford to lose
7. **Keep Adequate Capital**: Maintain sufficient capital to handle adverse moves and margin requirements

### Broker Requirements

- Futures trading typically requires a minimum account balance (usually $2,000-$5,000)
- Margin requirements vary by contract and broker
- Intraday margin is typically lower than overnight margin
- Margin calls can occur with rapid adverse price movements
